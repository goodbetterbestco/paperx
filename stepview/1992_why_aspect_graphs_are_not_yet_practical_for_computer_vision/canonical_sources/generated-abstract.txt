Generated abstract from paper content. This panel paper examines why aspect graphs, despite their elegant description of stable views, were not yet practical for computer vision systems. The critiques emphasize unresolved problems of scale, explosive graph size, indexing and matching, unreliable contour extraction, and the gap between topological definitions of aspect and features that can actually be recovered from images. The responses argue that broader, task-dependent notions of aspect, better feature selection, and viewer-centered representations organized around recoverable features may retain the value of precomputed views. Taken together, the discussion reframes aspect graphs less as a finished recognition solution than as a starting point for more usable appearance-based models.
